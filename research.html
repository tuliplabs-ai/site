Clusiana is our research program on the infrastructure AI runs on. Its first project is a cross-process GPU timing side channel that fingerprints the inference engine and the language model running on a co-located NVIDIA GPU — from an ordinary, unprivileged process sharing the same silicon. We publish the risk, the result, and the defense; the offensive method stays in the lab.

True to how we work, the single most useful artifact isn't the attack — it's a 140-line, standard-library audit tool a defender runs on their own infrastructure to measure whether their workload is identifiable from a co-tenant. Alongside it: a mitigation we measured honestly at roughly a quarter of throughput.

Scope, stated up front: this targets shared-GPU configurations where multiple unprivileged processes share one physical GPU (Docker --gpus all, MPS, some shared-tenant cloud offerings, WSL2). A dedicated single-tenant GPU — with no co-tenant to observe — is out of scope.

Published · grounding

GSAR — grounded answer verification

When an AI draws a conclusion, is it actually supported by the evidence it cites? Often not — every fact is real, but the conclusion over-reaches.

GSAR is our method for measuring that gap. It partitions an answer's claims into grounded, ungrounded, contradicted, and complementary; scores how well the whole is supported; and decides whether to proceed, regenerate, or replan — with six monotonicity and adversarial-robustness properties proven and locked under tests. It's published and peer-readable, and it is the engine behind the Tulip SDK: a security finding ships only when its evidence clears the bar.
